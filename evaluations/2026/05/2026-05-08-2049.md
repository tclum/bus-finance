## Hypothesis 2: Regional bank profitability is under pressure from deposit mix shift or loan-loss provisioning, despite strong employment data that should support credit quality.

**Category:** Profitability
**Confidence:** Medium

### Observation

WFC fell -4.45% on 1.66× average volume, the largest single-name decline in the S&P 500 today. BAC and C also declined -2.73% and -2.74% respectively, both on elevated volume (1.05× and 1.25×). XLF, the financial sector ETF, fell -0.60% on 0.74× volume, underperforming the S&P 500 by 144 bps. This selloff occurred on the same day Reuters reported "April jobs report shows continued strength" and "Strong US jobs data complicates any Warsh push for lower rates." Strong employment typically supports bank credit quality and should be positive for financials, yet the sector sold off. The divergence suggests either (1) margin compression from deposit repricing, (2) increased loan-loss provisioning despite strong macro data, or (3) market pricing in higher-for-longer rates that compress net interest margins on the back book.

### Why it matters

If regional banks are provisioning more aggressively despite strong employment, it signals credit stress in specific loan portfolios (commercial real estate, small business, auto) that the aggregate jobs data is masking. If instead the issue is deposit mix shift — customers moving from non-interest-bearing to interest-bearing accounts — then net interest margins are compressing and the profitability headwind persists until rates decline. The CFO needs to understand which dynamic is driving the selloff, because it shapes our own credit-risk assessment for any bank-financed inventory or receivables facilities, and it informs our view on whether regional banks remain viable counterparties for treasury operations.

### Recommended investigation

- Pull WFC, BAC, and C most recent 10-Q filings and compute the QoQ change in (1) net interest margin, (2) non-interest-bearing deposits as % of total deposits, and (3) loan-loss provision as % of total loans.
- Compare the above ratios to the same banks' 2023 and 2024 levels to identify whether the current quarter represents an inflection or continuation of a trend.
- Check whether any of the three banks reported earnings or pre-announced results in the last 48 hours; if so, pull the earnings release and identify the specific line item that missed consensus.
- Pull regional bank ETF (KRE) performance vs. large-cap bank ETF (XLF) over the last five days to determine whether the selloff is concentrated in regionals or broad-based across all financials.
- Review sell-side bank analyst reports from the last week for any commentary on deposit repricing or loan-loss reserve builds.
