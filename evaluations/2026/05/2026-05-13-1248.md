The CFO needs to know whether our pricing power allows us to pass through costs faster than peers, and whether our Q2 guidance already reflects this input-cost step-function or needs revision.

- Model a 140-bp annualized COGS inflation scenario with 30-day, 60-day, and 90-day price-adjustment lags; compute the implied operating margin compression for each lag assumption.

**Confidence:** Low

### Observation

### Why it matters

Small-caps typically carry higher leverage ratios and lower interest coverage than large-caps. If the market is pricing a Fed pause or hike due to sticky inflation, small-cap borrowing costs rise faster than large-cap costs, compressing liquidity and increasing default risk. The CFO needs to know whether our suppliers or customers are small-cap-heavy; if so, we face second-order credit risk even if our own balance sheet is strong. The divergence between XLF (large banks gaining) and ^RUT (small-caps lagging) is a classic liquidity-stress signal.

### Recommended investigation

- Pull the current ratio and quick ratio for the bottom quartile of the Russell 2000 by market cap; compare to the 2022 tightening cycle to gauge whether liquidity is deteriorating.
- Compute the median interest coverage ratio (EBIT / interest expense) for Russell 2000 components with >30% debt-to-assets; if coverage is <3.0×, a 100-bp rate increase creates refinancing stress.
- Review our customer and supplier credit exposure; if >20% of receivables or payables are to small-cap counterparties, model the impact of a 5% default rate.
- Check the spread between Russell 2000 forward P/E and S&P 500 forward P/E; if the spread is widening, the market is pricing a small-cap earnings recession.
