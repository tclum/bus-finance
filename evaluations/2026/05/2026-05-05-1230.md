If UPS is seeing parcel-volume deterioration, the second-order read is that e-commerce demand is softening faster than consensus expects, which has direct implications for our own logistics cost structure and any volume-based carrier contracts we have in place. If crack spreads are widening into May, it suggests either supply tightness (refinery outages, which we would have seen in the news) or demand strength (early summer driving season pull-forward). The CFO should care because rising gasoline prices feed through to our own logistics costs with a 30–60 day lag, and if this is a demand story rather than a supply story, it implies the consumer is stronger than the retail tape (XLY -0.77%, LOW -4.12%, HD -3.54%) would suggest — a contradiction worth resolving.

### Recommended investigation

- Compare MPC and PSX's gross refining margins (available in their 10-Qs under segment reporting) to the prior quarter and prior year; if margins are expanding, it confirms the crack-spread hypothesis.

### Why it matters

If LOW and HD are repricing for lower turnover, it suggests either that mortgage rates are biting harder than the headline data shows (because the effective rate for movers is higher than the average rate for all homeowners) or that home-equity lines of credit are tightening. The CFO should care because our own consumer-facing businesses (if any) are exposed to the same home-equity wealth effect, and if housing turnover is slowing, it will show up in our own sales data with a 1–2 quarter lag.

### Recommended investigation
