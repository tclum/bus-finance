## Hypothesis 4: The Iran war's supply shock is creating a two-tier market where energy-intensive sectors face margin compression while energy producers and AI-power infrastructure capture the upside, but the VIX's muted response suggests the market is underpricing the tail risk.

**Category:** Macro/Strategic
**Confidence:** Medium

### Observation

MarketWatch reported "The world lost nearly 1 billion barrels in oil supply over the past 75 days" with prices climbing nearly 50% since late February, yet XLE (energy sector) gained only +0.10% today on 0.50× volume—subdued relative to the supply shock magnitude. Reuters separately noted "Iran, Ukraine wars deliver worst hit in years to oil refining output," and MarketWatch warned "The Iran war could be a $300 billion shock—driving up mortgage rates and squeezing wages." Despite this, the VIX sits at 17.87, down -0.67% on the day, and the S&P 500 gained +0.58%. The divergence between the supply-shock headlines and the market's calm suggests either the impact is already priced or the market is underestimating second-order effects on margins for energy-intensive sectors (industrials XLI -0.42%, materials XLB -0.15%).

### Why it matters

If the market is underpricing the Iran war's margin impact, energy-intensive sectors (chemicals, metals, transportation) face a delayed repricing lower once Q2 earnings reveal the cost pass-through lag. For the CFO, this shapes our own cost-of-goods-sold forecasting: if oil-derived input costs have spiked 50% but our pricing power is limited, gross margin compression is inevitable. Conversely, if we have energy-infrastructure exposure (per Hypothesis 1's Ford example), we may be undervaluing that optionality. The $300 billion shock figure implies this is not a transient event—it is a structural shift that will persist into 2027.

### Recommended investigation

- Pull our own COGS breakdown and isolate the percentage attributable to oil-derived inputs (plastics, transportation fuel, petrochemicals); model a 50% sustained price increase and compute the gross margin impact under three scenarios: full pass-through, 50% pass-through, zero pass-through.
- Identify our top five energy-intensive suppliers and check their most recent earnings calls for commentary on cost inflation and pricing power; flag any that signal margin pressure.
- Compare XLE's muted +0.10% move against WTI crude's trailing 75-day performance (if available via Bloomberg) to gauge whether energy equities are lagging the commodity, which would suggest a catch-up trade or a structural disconnect.
- Review our hedging strategy for oil-linked costs and assess whether current contracts cover the next two quarters given the supply-shock persistence indicated by the headlines.
