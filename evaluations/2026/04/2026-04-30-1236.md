## Hypothesis 2: Energy's outperformance (+2.29% sector, +5.1% PSX, +4.0% MPC) is driven by refining margin expansion from Iran war supply disruptions, not crude price alone, creating a wedge between integrated majors and refiners.

**Category:** Profitability
**Confidence:** Medium

### Observation

XLE rallied +2.29% today and is up +4.40% over five days, leading all sector ETFs. Within energy, refiners PSX (+5.06%, 1.14× volume) and MPC (+3.96%, 1.07× volume) significantly outpaced integrated majors XOM (+2.73%, 0.65× volume) and CVX (+2.05%, 0.67× volume). Reuters headline "Stagflation risks stacking up as Iran war enters third month" explicitly ties the move to supply disruption, and a separate Reuters piece noted "Gold climbs from one-month low as dollar eases; investors assess Iran tensions." The refiner outperformance suggests the market is pricing crack spread expansion — the margin between crude input cost and refined product output price — rather than crude appreciation alone. Integrated majors benefit from higher crude prices but are less leveraged to refining margins.

### Why it matters

If crack spreads are widening due to refining capacity constraints (rather than crude scarcity), the profitability impact flows disproportionately to pure-play refiners, and the duration of the margin expansion depends on how long Iran supply remains offline. For a CFO managing energy procurement or fuel cost pass-through, this distinction matters: crude hedges protect against input cost, but crack spread volatility requires a different hedging structure. If our operations have significant diesel or jet fuel exposure, we may be underhedged for the refining margin component.

### Recommended investigation

- Pull PSX and MPC most recent quarterly gross refining margin per barrel (disclosed in 10-Q or earnings supplement) and compare to XOM and CVX's downstream segment margins; quantify the spread.
- Check the 3-2-1 crack spread (a standard refining margin proxy) over the last 30 days using EIA data; if it has widened materially, the hypothesis is confirmed.
- Model our fuel procurement costs under three scenarios: (1) crude +10%, crack spread flat; (2) crude flat, crack spread +20%; (3) both up 10%. Identify which scenario creates the largest P&L impact.
- Review our existing energy hedging book to confirm whether we have crack spread exposure or only crude price exposure; if the latter, evaluate whether adding refining margin hedges is cost-effective.

If GLP-1 margins are compressing despite volume growth, it signals that the category is entering a more competitive phase where scale no longer guarantees pricing power. For a CFO evaluating healthcare investments or partnerships, this matters because the unit economics of the category may be deteriorating faster than the revenue growth suggests. ### Recommended investigation
