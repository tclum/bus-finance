## C. Interpretation & Key Findings

### 1. Walmart Is Creating Significant Shareholder Value

EVA of **$10,090M** confirms that Walmart generates returns well above its cost of capital — this is the most important single finding in the analysis. For a company of Walmart's asset base ($236B), sustaining positive EVA is a genuine competitive achievement. MVA of **$257.8B** reflects the market's confidence that this value creation will continue: investors are willing to pay $4.45 for every $1.00 of book equity. Both metrics signal a well-managed, economically productive enterprise.

### 2. The Business Model Is Built on Volume, Not Margin

Walmart's profit margin is just **2.9%** — thin by any standard. But this is a deliberate strategic choice, not a weakness. The Everyday Low Price (EDLP) model sacrifices margin for volume. The result is asset turnover of **2.37x**, meaning Walmart generates $2.37 in revenue for every dollar of assets — among the highest in large-cap retail. The Du Pont decomposition makes this explicit: Walmart's ROA is not driven by fat margins, it is driven by relentless asset productivity. Any recommendation to simply "raise prices to improve margins" misunderstands the model.

### 3. Leverage Is Elevated but Well-Managed

Total debt ratio of **65.5%** and a leverage ratio of **3.17x** indicate that Walmart is significantly debt-financed. However, this is not a cause for alarm given the coverage ratios: Times Interest Earned of **8.5x** and Cash Coverage of **13.1x** provide substantial cushion over interest obligations. The debt burden ratio of **0.89x** confirms that nearly all after-tax operating income flows through to net income, meaning debt costs are not materially impairing profitability. Walmart's scale and cash flow predictability support a capital structure that would be concerning for a smaller or more cyclical company.

### 4. Negative Working Capital Is a Competitive Advantage

Current ratio of **0.79x** and net working capital of **−$15,984M** would trigger alarm in most industries. For Walmart, this is a deliberate and enviable feature of the business model. Walmart collects cash from customers immediately at point of sale, but pays suppliers on 30–60 day terms. This means suppliers are effectively financing Walmart's operations — a structural advantage that reflects Walmart's extraordinary purchasing power. The quick ratio of **0.20x** is similarly low, but this reflects Walmart's minimal receivables (collection period of just **4.4 days**), not financial distress.

### 5. Profitability Ratios Are Strong Across All Three Return Metrics

ROE of **20.5%**, ROA of **7.6%**, and ROC of **14.4%** all reflect strong returns for a company of Walmart's size and asset intensity. ROC of 14.4% versus a cost of capital of 5.7% confirms that every dollar invested in the business generates returns nearly 2.5x the required hurdle rate. These ratios hold across both start-of-year and average denominators (ROA avg: 7.3%; ROE avg: 20.2%), confirming consistency rather than a one-time artifact.

## D. Du Pont Analysis Discussion

Walmart's ROE of **20.5%** decomposes as follows under the Du Pont framework:

| Component | Value | Interpretation |
|-----------|-------|----------------|
| Leverage | 3.17x | Amplifies returns via debt financing |
| Asset Turnover | 2.37x | Very high — core competitive strength |
| Operating Profit Margin | 3.2% | Intentionally thin |
| Debt Burden | 0.89x | Minimal drag from interest costs |
| **Du Pont ROE** | **21.5%** | Approximates direct ROE of 20.5% ✓ |

The decomposition tells a clear story: **Walmart's ROE is not a margin story — it is a turnover and leverage story.** The operating profit margin of 3.2% is near the bottom of large-cap retail. What elevates ROE to 20.5% is the combination of exceptional asset turnover (2.37x) and moderate financial leverage (3.17x).

This has an important strategic implication: efforts to improve ROE should focus on maintaining or improving asset productivity (e.g., inventory management, store throughput, e-commerce fulfillment efficiency) rather than attempting to expand margins in ways that might conflict with the EDLP brand promise. Leverage is already elevated; further debt-financed buybacks would increase ROE arithmetically but at the cost of balance sheet flexibility.

The minor difference between Du Pont ROE (21.5%) and direct ROE (20.5%) reflects the use of blended denominators in the decomposition versus start-of-year equity in the direct calculation — a normal modeling artifact, not an error.

## E. Strategic Recommendations

### 1. Communicate EVA Proactively to Investors
**Data:** EVA = $10,090M; cost of capital = 5.7%; ROC = 14.4%

Walmart generates $10.1B in economic value added annually — a figure that does not appear in standard earnings releases and is not widely discussed in investor communications. Given the current market-to-book ratio of 4.45x, investors are already pricing in continued value creation, but explicitly reporting EVA in earnings calls and investor day materials would reinforce the narrative and differentiate Walmart from peers whose returns do not clear the cost of capital hurdle. This is a low-cost, high-impact investor relations action.

### 2. Accelerate Inventory Turnover in Lower-Velocity Categories
**Data:** Days in inventory = 40.9 days; inventory balance = $44,435M

While 40.9 days is reasonable for a retailer of Walmart's breadth, the sheer scale means each day of inventory reduction frees approximately $1.08B in working capital (daily COGS = $1,081M). A targeted program to reduce days in inventory by 3–5 days in lower-velocity general merchandise categories (apparel, seasonal goods, home furnishings) could unlock $3–5B in cash without impacting grocery or consumables where stock-outs carry higher customer satisfaction risk.

### 3. Use Debt Capacity Selectively for Strategic Acquisitions or Buybacks
**Data:** Times Interest Earned = 8.5x; total debt ratio = 65.5%; cash from operations = $25,255M

Walmart's coverage ratios indicate meaningful remaining debt capacity despite an elevated total debt ratio. With operating cash flow of $25.3B and interest expense of only $2.4B, the company could service additional debt comfortably. Management should evaluate whether strategic acquisitions in e-commerce, supply chain technology, or international markets would generate returns above the 5.7% WACC threshold. If no such opportunities meet the hurdle, disciplined share repurchases (Walmart repurchased $5.7B in FY2020) remain appropriate given the strong EVA track record.

### 4. Monitor ASC 842 Lease Impact on Leverage Metrics Going Forward
**Data:** Lease ROU assets added $14.8B to FY2020 balance sheet; prior-year not restated

The adoption of the new lease standard in FY2020 added $21.8B in assets and $15.1B in liabilities that were off-balance-sheet in FY2019. This mechanically increased the total debt ratio and leverage ratio without any change in underlying economic obligation. As the lease standard is now fully reflected, FY2021 forward comparisons will be clean — but any multi-year trend analysis presented to the board should footnote this structural break to avoid misleading comparisons. Covenant calculations in existing credit agreements should also be reviewed to confirm they use pre-ASC-842 metrics where applicable.

## G. Executive Justification

**Financial Health:** Walmart enters FY2021 in sound financial condition. EVA of $10.1B and ROC of 14.4% confirm that the company generates returns substantially above its cost of capital, the most fundamental test of financial health for a public company. Coverage ratios (TIE: 8.5x; Cash Coverage: 13.1x) provide ample protection against earnings volatility.

**Operational Efficiency:** Asset turnover of 2.37x is the engine of Walmart's model. The receivables collection period of 4.4 days reflects near-frictionless cash conversion from customers. Days in inventory of 40.9 days reflects solid management across a $44B inventory base, though targeted reductions in slow-moving categories could free several billion in additional liquidity.

**Capital Structure:** At 65.5% total debt, Walmart is meaningfully leveraged, but the combination of stable operating cash flows ($25.3B), investment-grade credit ratings, and comfortable coverage ratios justifies this structure. The leverage amplifies ROE to 20.5% without creating financial distress risk.

**Liquidity Position:** The current ratio of 0.79x and negative NWC of −$16.0B reflect Walmart's structural advantage over suppliers, not a liquidity problem. With $9.5B in cash and $25.3B in annual operating cash flow, Walmart's liquidity is robust in economic substance even if the conventional ratios appear concerning.

**Value Creation Track Record:** MVA of $257.8B means the market values Walmart's future earnings power at $258B above the book value of invested capital. This is the cumulative recognition of decades of value creation above the cost of capital. It is a track record that management should protect carefully — particularly in capital allocation decisions where return on incremental investment must continue to clear the 5.7% WACC hurdle.

**Accounting and Reporting Implications:** The ASC 842 lease adoption in FY2020 represents a material structural change to the balance sheet that should be disclosed clearly in all trend comparisons. Additionally, Walmart's embedded D&A treatment (not reported as a separate income statement line) means standard ratio templates must be adapted to source depreciation from the Cash Flow Statement — a detail that can cause significant errors if overlooked in automated or AI-assisted modeling workflows.
